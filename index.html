Why This Matters to You

Over 542,000 robots are installed globally every year, which is double what it was just a decade ago. Whether you're planning to be a nurse, mechanic, graphic designer, or retail worker, automation will change how you do your job. Even part time work, such as cashier positions and warehouse jobs, are undergoing a significant transformation right now.

Think about a fast food restaurant where kiosks are already replacing cashiers. Or think about a warehouse where robots move products while humans just monitor screens. Or a hospital where AI reads X-rays faster than radiologists.This isn't some distant future, it's happening now. Companies are installing over half a million robots every single year. The workplace you enter will look nothing like the one now.

But here's what most people ignore: automation doesn't just destroy jobs, it transforms them. The question isn't whether automation will affect your career; it's whether you'll be the one replaced by it or the one managing it.

When Robots Create Jobs

When robots increased 4x in Europe between 1995 and 2015 (from 100,000 to 400,000 units), employment didn't crash, it grew by 1.3%.

Why? Because robots replaced repetitive tasks, not entire jobs. A car factory that automated welding still needed humans to program the robots, maintain them, handle quality control, and make judgment calls when things went wrong. Workers who adapted moved into higher skilled roles, often with better pay and safer working conditions.

The difference? European workers and companies invested in training. They didn't fight automation, they learned to work alongside it. The workers who lost their jobs were the ones who refused to adapt. The ones who thrived were those who saw the robots as tools, not replacements.

Robotic Automation

There are currently approximately 4.66 million industrial robots operating worldwide. A new robot is installed every 58 seconds, and by 2028, that is expected to increase to one every 45 seconds.

What are Robots?

RPA, or Robotic Process Automation systems are machines designed to carry out tasks automatically by following programmed instructions. Unlike traditional machines that perform only one fixed function, robots can be reprogrammed to handle different tasks. Robotics systems are often used with sensors, cameras, and software to help them interact with their environment. Robots typically perform what are known as the three Ds: tasks that are dull, dirty, and dangerous. These include organizing products in warehouses, working in hazardous environments, and handling heavy or unsafe materials. By taking on this type of work, robots increase efficiency and safety but they also change how human labor is used. As robots move beyond basic factory work and into more complex roles, their influence on everyday jobs continues to grow.

Fields RPA is changing

Robotic automation is affecting every industry.

Manufacturing

Robots excel where humans struggle: welding with perfect precision for hours without fatigue, working in extreme heat near molten metal, working with toxic fumes, and lifting massive parts that would injure workers. 38% of all industrial robots work in automotive manufacturing alone, with 7.5 robots per 1,000 workers handling the dangerous, repetitive tasks humans used to risk their bodies doing.

Warehouses

Robotic systems are revolutionizing warehouses. Amazon's Kiva and Walmart's R5 robots move entire shelves of products directly to workers, eliminating the 15+ miles employees used to walk each day. Instead of workers destroying their backs lifting boxes, they stand at stations scanning items while robots do the heavy lifting. These systems work 24/7, sort products faster than humans ever could, and have transformed warehouse work from backbreaking physical labor into system management. It's the same ammount of workers, just now with less pain.

Agriculture

Robotic systems with 3D vision software are transforming farms and greenhouses. Companies like Robovision have developed robots that can identify exactly which plant needs harvesting, where to plant bulbs with millimeter precision, and navigate through crops without damaging them. These robots handle the repetitive bending, lifting, and sorting that destroys workers' backs during long harvest seasons. They work in extreme weather conditions, blazing heat or freezing cold, that would exhaust human laborers, while allowing farmers to focus on crop planning, quality decisions, and managing operations rather than spending 12-hour days in the fields.

The Three Ds

Robots perform best in enviornments that are dull, dirty, or dangerous environments where humans shouldn't have to work. They defuse bombs, clean toxic waste sites, explore collapsed buildings during rescue operations, and work in extreme temperatures that would kill humans within minutes. In industrial settings, robots handle carcinogenic chemicals, work inside furnaces reaching thousands of degrees, and enter confined spaces filled with hazardous gases. These aren't jobs being "stolen", they're jobs that were sending workers home with cancer, chronic injuries, or not sending them home at all. Robots take the risks so humans don't have to.
