Understanding the Impact on Your Career

Over 542,000 robots are installed globally every year, which is more than double what it was just a decade ago. Whether you're planning to be a doctor, mechanic, retail worker, or a police officer, automation will change how you do your job.

Look at fast food restaurants, where kiosks are already replacing cashiers. Or warehouses, where robots move products while humans just monitor screens. Or even hospitals, where AI systems read X-rays faster than radiologists. This isn't some distant future, it's happening now. Companies are installing over half a million robots every single year. The workplace you enter will look nothing like the one that exists now.

People often focus on the jobs automation replaces, not the new opportunities it can create. The question isn't whether automation will affect your career, it's how you'll adapt to it.

When Robots Create Jobs

When robotic installations increased by over four times in Europe between the years of 1995 and 2015 (from under 100,000 to over 400,000 units), employment didn't crash, it grew by 1.3%.

Robots are meant to replace repetitive tasks, not entire jobs. For example, an automotive plant that automated welding and assembly systems will still need humans to program the robotic systems, maintain them, handle quality control, and make decisions when things go wrong.

European workers and companies invested in training, learning to work with automation instead of fighting it. Workers that lost jobs were people who refused to adapt and saw automation as a replacement instead of tools to boost productivity.

Robotic Automation

There are approximately 4.66 million industrial robots currently operating worldwide. A new robot is installed every 58 seconds, and by 2028, that is expected to increase to one every 45 seconds.

What are Robots?

RPA systems, or Robotic Process Automation systems, are machines designed to carry out tasks automatically by following programmed instructions. Unlike traditional machines that perform only one fixed function, robots can be reprogrammed to handle different tasks. Robotics systems are often used with sensors, cameras, and software to help them interact with their environment. Robots typically perform what are known as the three Ds: tasks that are dull, dirty, and dangerous. These include organizing products in warehouses, working in hazardous environments, and handling heavy or unsafe materials. By taking on this type of work, robots increase efficiency and safety, but they also change how human labor is used. As robots move beyond basic factory work and into more complex roles, their influence on everyday jobs continues to grow.

Fields RPA is changing

Manufacturing

Robots excel at tasks that humans struggle with. For example, robots can weld with perfect precision for hours without fatigue, work in extreme heat near molten metal, and lift massive parts that would injure workers. 38% of all industrial robotic installations are in the automotive manufacturing industries, with approximately 7.5 robots per 1,000 workers.

Warehouses

Robotic systems are helping revolutionize warehouses. Amazon's Kiva and Walmart's R5 units can move entire shelves of products directly to workers. This eliminates the 15+ miles employees used to need to walk each day. Instead of workers needing to lift heavy packages multiple times a day, they can stand at stations scanning items while robots do the heavy lifting for them. These systems work 24/7 and can sort products faster than humans ever could, helping to increase productivity in warehouses.

Agriculture

Robotic systems that have 3D vision software are transforming farms and greenhouses. Companies like Robovision have developed robots that are able to identify what plants need to be harvested, where to plant bulbs with precision up to a millimeter, and are able to navigate through crops without damaging them. These robotic systems handle the repetitive bending, lifting, and sorting that destroys workers' backs during long harvest seasons. They can work in extreme weather conditions like blazing heat or freezing cold that would exhaust human laborers, allowing farmers to focus on planning what crops to grow, quality control, and managing operations rather than spending full days in the fields.

The Three Ds

Robots perform best in environments that are dull, dirty, or dangerous, where humans would struggle to work. Robots can defuse bombs, clean toxic waste sites, explore collapsed buildings during rescue operations, and can work in extreme temperatures that would kill humans within minutes. In industrial settings, robotic systems handle carcinogenic chemicals, work inside furnaces that can reach temperatures that are thousands of degrees, and enter confined spaces filled with hazardous gases. These aren't jobs that are being "stolen," they're jobs that were sending workers home with cancer, chronic injuries, or not sending them home at all. Robots are taking these risks so humans don't have to.
